World-model study · single- & multi-body · 10-seed

When does structure beat
a learned world model?

A physics-grounded predictor versus a black-box neural net, on the same world. I ran it to break the idea, not flatter it — over many seeds, against a genuinely strong baseline, and then I removed the toy: one body became many, interacting and colliding. The wins mostly survived. One didn't. Here is the honest map of where structure helps and where it doesn't.

Live · lead the target

Both models aim by predicting the future

Each model must guess where the target will be, then fire a straight shot to meet it. Both were trained only on slow targets — the teal band on the slider. Push the speed past that band and the black-box aims short: it cannot emit motion faster than it has seen. The structured model just integrates the physics, so speed is no obstacle. This one mechanism — the integrator extrapolates, the black-box can't — is the thread through every win below.

The scorecard · one body

Structure wins in the data-poor, novel, changing corner

Two models, same 2D world (gravity + drag), same data, same budget. The black-box is tuned — not a strawman. Every number is the mean over 10 seeds.

StructureLittle data. Sits on the oracle floor from ~8 samples; the tuned black-box needs ~100+ to match it.
StructureFast reaction to novelty. 4–8× more accurate intercepting a never-seen fast target with a split second to react.
StructureAdapting to a changing world. Settles ~2× lower error after the wind shifts — it re-learns only the force.
Black-boxRich, on-distribution data. Given dense in-distribution data, a net that has memorized the operator matches or beats the learned structured model. No free accuracy from structure here.
The de-toy · many bodies

Then I removed the toy

The obvious objection to a one-body world: of course a smooth single force is easy to bake in. So I made it hard. Now there are many bodies, mutually attracting, with elastic collisions — a force that is genuinely relational and event-driven. And I gave the black-box the same relational structure (a permutation-invariant net that reads every pair), so the only thing left to compare is the physics itself.

The single-body wins transferred — on the axes where the win comes from the integrator. And one didn't.

StructureData efficiency — up to ~9×. Structure's error floor sits ~8× below the black-box's, even against a relational baseline.
StructureOOD speed — 2.4×. Train slow, test fast: the integrator extrapolates Δpos = v·dt for free.
StructureNon-stationary — 1.5×. Shift the attraction strength mid-stream; the hybrid re-learns only the pair force. Sharpest when the change makes the world harder.
BreaksOOD by body count. Train on 6 bodies, test on 12 and structure's edge is gone (0.9×). More bodies means the learned force must extrapolate to denser configurations — and the integrator can't rescue a wrong force.
Where it breaks

The one honest loss

Trained on 6 bodies, tested on more. The bar is the black-box's error divided by the structured model's — above the line, structure wins; below it, the black-box does. Watch it cross.

Instrument · non-stationary world

Adapting when the rules change

The world runs calm, then the attraction strength shifts — twice. Both models spike; neither saw it coming. Then they learn online from what they observe. Changing a force parameter alters only the force, so the structured model re-learns a small correction and keeps its integrator and collisions intact; the black-box must repair its whole entangled operator.

Method

How it was run

Structured model = a fixed kinematic integrator (plus, in the multi-body world, an exact collision resolver) with a small learned force plugged in (state → acceleration); it gets the physics form for free and learns only the unknown force. In the multi-body world the force is a permutation-invariant interaction net that reads every pair. Black-box = the same relational net, but mapping state → next state directly, with no integrator and no collision structure — a genuinely strong baseline, not a strawman. Same data, same budget; every headline is a mean over 10 seeds. Everything is a self-contained C# console program with hand-rolled nets and no dependencies. One honest cost: the structured model integrates at fine resolution, ~16× the black-box's compute — structure isn't free, it's just more general. The demo above reproduces the out-of-distribution mechanism faithfully in the browser.

Take it apart

This isn't a product. It's a result — posted with the loss in it, because a result you only ever confirm isn't a result. I'd genuinely like to hear from people working on world-models: where would you expect this to hold, and where to break?
