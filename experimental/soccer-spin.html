closer to the true landing than the black box, from just 15% of the flight — measured over many kicks in the full simulation (structured 0.46 m vs black-box 5.9 m). Inferring the spin is three numbers, so almost no data is needed.

Why it works: the structured model isn't memorising trajectories — it's handed the kinematics and only has to identify the one hidden thing it can't see, the spin. Three numbers, fit from the observed arc, and the physics draws the rest. The black box has no notion of spin; from a short fragment it can't tell that the ball will keep curving, so it flies on and misses.

Method

How the demo works

The true flight is gravity + quadratic drag + the Magnus force (F ∝ ω × v) integrated in 3D. The structured predictor is given the physics form and infers the spin ω from the revealed fragment by a one-shot least-squares fit to the observed accelerations, then integrates forward. The black box has no spin state, so its best continuation from the fragment is the spin-free path. In the full study the structured force is learned (a small neural net, no hand-coded Magnus) and still lands within ~0.5 m in its training range; the browser uses the analytic force to run live, so here it stays sharp even far out on the spin slider — with the learned force, pushing the spin well past what it trained on makes it slip too (a few metres), and the study reports that honestly. Same mechanism, same conclusion.
