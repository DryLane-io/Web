A model-predictive controller flies it, planning thrust and gimbal by simulating the future through a learned world model. Adding attitude makes the state richer and the control harder, but it doesn't change the lesson: a model that learns the physics from a handful of samples is accurate enough to plan through, so you can act. A black box that must learn the whole map — now including how it rotates — needs far more before it can be trusted at the controls.

Method

How the demo works

The rocket is a 2-D rigid body: position, velocity, tilt and spin. You command thrust (along the body) and a gimbal angle that torques it. The controller is real model-predictive control over both. The structured model is the true rigid-body dynamics with a known actuation model — its one unknown, the passive aerodynamic force, is learned accurately from almost nothing. The black box is that physics plus an error — in thrust and especially in how the gimbal rotates it — that shrinks as training data grows, standing in for a learned 6-D map that's inaccurate when data is scarce. In the full study both are neural nets; the browser runs the mechanism live.
